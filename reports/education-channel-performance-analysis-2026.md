# PBS Wisconsin Education Channel — YouTube Performance Analysis
**Prepared:** March 2026
**Data source:** YouTube Analytics SQLite database (as of March 6, 2026)
**Scope:** PBS Wisconsin Education channel (253 videos) vs. PBS Wisconsin main channel (4,359 videos)

---

## Executive Summary

The PBS Wisconsin Education channel (253 videos, 614K total views) is significantly underperforming its potential — not because of content quality, but because of structural format and publishing decisions that work against YouTube's current algorithm. The channel's best-performing content reveals a clear path forward: its short-form educational clips (The Look Back, The Ways, aka Teacher) are generating sustained discovery-mode velocity, while its long-form uploads sit mostly dormant. A targeted pivot toward 4–12 minute educational shorts with strong search optimization could realistically double the channel's monthly view count within 6–9 months without requiring new production.

**Key findings at a glance:**

| Metric | Education Channel | Main Channel | Gap |
|--------|-------------------|--------------|-----|
| Videos in database | 253 | 4,359 | — |
| Total views | 614,032 | 17,273,885 | 28x |
| Avg views/video | 2,427 | 3,963 | 39% below |
| Avg views/day (velocity) | 4.2 | 6.2 | 32% below |
| Avg engagement rate | 1.73% | 1.93% | Comparable |
| Avg video duration | 11.7 min | 16.8 min | — |

The engagement gap is smaller than the views gap suggests — the Education channel is not producing poor content, it is producing content in formats and durations that limit algorithmic distribution.

## Part 2: Format Analysis — Where the Channel Actually Works

### 2.1 Duration Buckets

This is the most diagnostic finding in the dataset:

| Duration | Videos | Avg Views | Avg Views/Day | Avg Engagement |
|----------|--------|-----------|---------------|----------------|
| 0–1 min (Shorts) | 60 | 904 | 11.4 | 2.35% |
| 1–8 min | 127 | 3,556 | 2.3 | 1.54% |
| 8–15 min | 31 | 345 | 1.5 | 2.18% |
| 15–28 min | 4 | 144 | 0.12 | 0.59% |
| 28–60 min | 17 | 1,409 | 0.71 | 0.84% |
| 60+ min | 14 | 5,207 | 2.6 | 1.15% |

The 8–28 minute range — which the research identifies as the algorithmic sweet spot for educational longform — is the Education channel's worst-performing segment. Only 35 videos fall in this range, and they average 258 views each. This is a critical structural gap.

The 60+ minute bucket performs better on raw average views (5,207) because it contains evergreen anchor content like the Ladson-Billings lectures and Educator Connection LIVE sessions that accumulated views over years. But their views/day velocity (2.6) reveals they have largely stopped growing.

Compare this to the main PBS Wisconsin channel, where the 28–60 minute bucket averages 7,594 views per video — 5x higher than the Education channel's best-performing duration bracket.

**The interpretation:** The Education channel lacks the middle layer. It has short clips (under 1 min) and long lectures (60+ min), but almost nothing in the 8–28 minute range where YouTube's satisfaction-weighted algorithm actively promotes educational content. Per the algorithm report, educational content averages 42% audience retention versus 24% platform-wide — an advantage that only materializes when videos are structured for that range.

### 2.2 The Look Back — A Format Case Study

The Look Back is the Education channel's most strategically significant series. It has 52 videos on the Education channel with a 7.4 views/day average — the highest velocity of any show on the channel, edging out The Ways (7.2).

But the Look Back exists in two distinct formats, and the performance difference is striking:

| Format | Videos | Avg Views | Avg Views/Day | Avg Engagement |
|--------|--------|-----------|---------------|----------------|
| Short clips (<90 seconds) | 30 | 1,491 | 22.2 | 1.89% |
| Long-form episodes (>90 seconds) | 26 | 344 | 1.7 | 2.06% |

The short clips generate **13x higher daily velocity** than the long-form versions. The top-performing Look Back video — "Why does Wisconsin have a George Washington portrait?" — is 46 seconds long and generates 38 views/day, more than any other Education channel video by velocity.

The channel appears to be running a parallel strategy: uploading both short clips (hashtag-optimized, search-friendly) and longer companion episodes (9–10 minutes) covering the same topics. The data is unambiguous about which format is driving growth. The QWERTY typewriter series from late 2025 / early 2026 illustrates this well: the short clip "QWERTY's Wisconsin history" gets 21 views/day while the 9.9-minute companion "QWERTY: How Wisconsin Holds the Keys to Typing History" gets 4.2 views/day.

**This does not mean abandoning long-form.** The long-form episodes have higher engagement rates (2.06% vs. 1.89%) and generate total watch time. But as discovery vehicles, the short clips are substantially more effective on this channel at its current subscriber base.

### 2.3 The Ways — The Channel's Best Asset

The Ways (14 videos, 203K total views) is the Education channel's strongest series by every metric: highest average views (14,509), solid velocity (7.2 views/day), and a 4–5 minute format that aligns with both YouTube discovery patterns and educational content consumption behavior.

All 14 videos cluster in the 3.8–5.5 minute range — exactly the length where YouTube can recommend content to non-subscribers without requiring a major time commitment. The series demonstrates that short-form educational documentary, not long-form lecture, is the channel's natural format advantage.

The top performers in the series are still accumulating views 6 years after publication (2020 uploads averaging 15+ views/day), which signals genuine search-intent discovery rather than promotional traffic. These videos are being found by students, teachers, and curious viewers conducting searches related to Indigenous language revitalization, cultural practices, and Wisconsin history.

**The gap:** No new Ways content has been added to the Education channel in the dataset. If this series went dormant, restoring or extending it would be a high-priority action.

## Part 3: How Education Content Compares to the Main Channel

### 3.1 Overall Performance Gap

The Education channel generates 28x fewer views than the main channel from 17x fewer videos — meaning it is slightly less efficient per video, but not dramatically so. The deeper problem is that the channel is not growing.

The main channel's strongest duration bucket (28–60 min, avg 7,594 views/video) is the Education channel's second-weakest. This likely reflects the difference in channel authority: the main PBS Wisconsin channel has significantly more subscriber history, watch signals, and algorithmic credibility. The Education channel cannot simply copy main channel format choices and expect comparable results.

### 3.2 University Place: The Main Channel's Educational Anchor

University Place (572 videos, 2.7M total views, avg 4,715 views/video) is the main channel's educational workhorse. It sits on the main channel, not the Education channel, which creates an interesting structural question: should the Education channel be targeting the same audience University Place serves, or a genuinely different one?

University Place averages 51 minutes per video and 2.9 views/day — primarily serving a self-directed adult learner audience who arrive with intent. The Education channel's short-form content targets a different mode of discovery: algorithmic recommendation and search-driven casual discovery among younger viewers. These are complementary, not competitive.

### 3.3 Archival Performance

The Education channel's strongest archival content (3+ years old, ranked by velocity) continues to outperform much of its recent uploads:

| Video | Views | Views/Day | Published |
|-------|-------|-----------|-----------|
| Adaptation and Mitigation (Climate Wisconsin) | 75,544 | 34.1 | Feb 2020 |
| Building Culturally Relevant Schools (Ladson-Billings) | 52,098 | 25.1 | Jun 2020 |
| Ableism (aka Teacher) | 31,352 | 21.5 | Mar 2022 |
| Prayers in a Song (The Ways) | 38,796 | 17.5 | Feb 2020 |
| Powwow Trail (The Ways) | 36,234 | 16.4 | Feb 2020 |

These videos are search-engine anchors. The Climate Wisconsin "Adaptation and Mitigation" video averaging 34 views/day after 6 years is likely receiving curriculum-driven traffic — teachers assigning it or students finding it through Google/YouTube searches. This pattern is the Education channel's structural advantage: once a high-quality educational video enters a curriculum context or becomes a reference resource, it generates compounding views indefinitely.

**The implication:** The Education channel should optimize for search discoverability above algorithmic recommendation. The archival velocity data suggests this is where the channel's audience actually comes from.

## Part 4: Engagement Quality

### 4.1 Engagement Rate Distribution

| Engagement Rate | Videos | Avg Views |
|-----------------|--------|-----------|
| <0.5% | 61 | 726 |
| 0.5–1% | 49 | 5,590 |
| 1–2% | 73 | 3,263 |
| 2–3% | 31 | 878 |
| 3%+ | 39 | 781 |

This pattern is counterintuitive: the videos with the highest engagement rates (3%+) have the lowest average views. This is a common pattern in educational content where niche or specialized videos attract smaller but highly engaged audiences. The low-engagement, high-view videos (0.5–1% bucket with 5,590 avg views) represent the channel's evergreen discovery content — broad appeal, lower comment/like density.

### 4.2 Shorts Performance

The Education channel has 60 videos at or under 1 minute (primarily YouTube Shorts), averaging 904 views each with 11.4 views/day and 2.35% engagement — above the channel average. Shorts appear to be working as discovery vehicles but have a structural limitation: Shorts revenue is substantially lower than regular video CPMs, and Shorts viewers have been shown to convert to regular video viewers at relatively low rates (per the audience report).

The Look Back's short clips appear to occupy a hybrid zone: many are 45–90 seconds (technically Shorts territory or near it), hashtag-optimized, and functioning as teaser content that links to longer companion episodes. This is a reasonable strategy, but the data shows those companion episodes are not capturing much of the short-clip audience.

## Part 5: Strategic Recommendations

### Priority 1 — Fill the 8–15 Minute Gap (Immediate)

The Education channel has almost no content in the 8–15 minute range (31 videos averaging 345 views each). This is the primary format gap to close. The research confirms this range crosses the 8-minute mid-roll ad threshold, aligns with YouTube's satisfaction-weighted algorithm, and is where educational content performs best (42% avg retention vs. 24% platform-wide).

**What this means in practice:**
- New The Look Back episodes should target 8–12 minutes per episode, not the current 9–10 minute durations that may be falling just outside optimization zones. Focus on keyword-rich topics with search intent (Wisconsin history, curriculum-adjacent subjects).
- Where the short clip format is working (under 90 seconds), maintain it as a companion but build the long-form companion toward 10–12 minutes, not the current 9–10 that may lack the depth YouTube's algorithm rewards.
- Revive or extend aka Teacher in the 8–12 minute range. "Ableism" (6 minutes, 31K views, 21.5 views/day) and "LGBTQ Bullying" (5.2 minutes, 11.9K views) are performing well at shorter lengths — a new series in the 8–12 minute range with strong search-intent titles could exceed this benchmark.

### Priority 2 — Invest in Chapter Markup for All Existing Long-Form Content

For the 14 videos over 60 minutes and the 17 videos in the 28–60 minute range, chapters (timestamp markers in the description) are non-negotiable per the production research. These videos currently receive 0.71–2.6 views/day. Adding chapters:
- Makes videos navigable without linear completion, improving average retention signals
- Generates search snippets on both YouTube and Google for each chapter section
- Is zero additional production cost — it requires only a description edit

Prioritize the Educator Connection LIVE and Building Background Knowledge videos first, as they represent the channel's highest-stakes long-form content.

### Priority 3 — Keyword-Optimize Titles and Descriptions for Search-Driven Discovery

The archival velocity data shows that the Education channel's best content is being found through search, not algorithmic recommendation. The implication is that SEO optimization will have more impact here than on the main channel.

**Specific actions:**
- Audit titles of The Ways, Wisconsin Biographies, and Wisconsin First Nations videos. Most use the format "Title | Show Name" — consider whether the title front-loads the search keyword the video answers. "Prayers in a Song: Learning Language Through Hip-Hop | The Ways" is well-titled; "Clan Mother: Healing the Community | The Ways" could be more specific about the cultural context.
- Add 150–200 word descriptions with relevant keywords (grade level, subject area, curriculum standard if applicable) for all educational content. These descriptions function as SEO signals for both YouTube and Google.
- Tag videos with educator-facing tags: "classroom video," "middle school," "high school," the relevant subject area, and Wisconsin-specific terms.

### Priority 4 — Treat Climate Wisconsin as a Growth Anchor

"Adaptation and Mitigation | Climate Wisconsin" is the Education channel's single most-viewed video (75,544 views, 34 views/day after 6 years). This is not a random hit — it reflects sustained search demand for climate curriculum content. With 9 total videos in Climate Wisconsin averaging 8,647 views (the second-highest of any show), this series has a demonstrably active search audience.

Per the research reports, the grant landscape includes new FCC caption requirements in September 2026, and MacArthur/Ford/Knight emergency commitments totaling $36.5M include environmental journalism and education as priority areas. A new Climate Wisconsin series developed with YouTube-native production (8–15 min episodes, chapters, keyword-optimized titles, professional captions) would simultaneously serve the search audience, qualify for grant consideration, and demonstrate digital audience development to funders.

### Priority 5 — Build a Curriculum Discovery Layer

The Education channel's strongest archival content survives on curriculum-driven traffic, yet there is no visible infrastructure connecting the content to teachers. This is a missed amplification opportunity.

**Options (low to medium effort):**
- Add grade level, subject area, and curriculum alignment notes to video descriptions. This improves searchability with teacher-specific queries ("Wisconsin history for 5th grade," "Indigenous language video for classroom").
- Create playlists organized by grade level and subject area rather than only by show name. Playlists increase session duration by 29–77% per the audience research.
- If PBS Wisconsin Education has a teacher newsletter or educator outreach program, each new education video should be included in that communication with a direct link and suggested curriculum context.

### Priority 6 — Evaluate the Dual-Channel Architecture

An honest strategic question: does maintaining a separate PBS Wisconsin Education channel serve the audience better than consolidating education content on the main channel, or creating an explicit Education playlist on the main channel?

**Arguments for the current separation:** The Education channel can be positioned as a curated classroom resource. Educators searching for reliable curriculum content may prefer a dedicated channel to a general news/documentary channel.

**Arguments against:** The Education channel receives less algorithmic authority than the main channel. University Place (on the main channel) has 572 videos and 2.7M views without a separate channel. Education channel videos may be disadvantaged in algorithmic distribution relative to identical content on the main channel.

This question cannot be fully answered from the analytics data alone — it depends on how PBS Wisconsin Education is being promoted to educators, whether the channel has classroom-specific features enabled, and whether the subscriber base is meaningfully different from the main channel's. This warrants investigation before any structural change.
